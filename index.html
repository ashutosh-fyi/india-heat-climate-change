+1.2°C

India has already warmed over 1°C above pre-industrial levels. Even small increases in average temperature have outsized consequences for 1.4 billion people. Here is what the data means in practice.

☀️

Deadly Heat Waves

India experienced its longest recorded heat wave in 2024 at 40+ consecutive days. Heat-related mortality has risen sharply — extreme heat days (above 40°C) have increased by over 55% since the 1980s. By 2050, over 600 million Indians could face regular lethal heat conditions.

24,000+ heat deaths since 1992
Source: Lancet Countdown 2024, IMD records
🌾

Agriculture Under Threat

Every 1°C rise reduces wheat yields by 6–7% and rice yields by up to 10% in key growing regions. India's agricultural GDP — which supports ~42% of the workforce — faces losses of $15–25 billion per year by mid-century from heat stress, erratic monsoons, and soil degradation.

6–7% wheat yield loss per 1°C
Source: IPCC AR6 WG2, ICAR studies
💧

Water Crisis

Himalayan glaciers — the source of the Ganges, Indus, and Brahmaputra — have lost 40% of their area since the Little Ice Age and retreat is accelerating. Meanwhile, groundwater tables are falling in Punjab, Haryana, and Rajasthan. By 2030, India's water demand is projected to be twice the available supply.

40% Himalayan glacier area lost
Source: ICIMOD Hindu Kush Himalaya Assessment
🌊

Monsoon Disruption

Warming is making the Indian monsoon more erratic: dry spells are longer, but when rain comes, it is more intense. Extreme rainfall events over central India have tripled since 1950. This means more floods and more droughts in the same year — devastating for farmers who depend on predictable rain.

3x increase in extreme rain events
Source: Roxy et al., Science 2017
🌎

Sea Level Rise

Sea levels along India's coast are rising at 1.7 mm/year, accelerating to 3.3 mm/year in recent decades. Mumbai, Chennai, and Kolkata — home to 46 million people — face significant flooding risk. A 1-metre rise by 2100 would submerge large parts of the Sundarbans, Kerala's coast, and Gujarat's low-lying areas.

3.3 mm/yr coastal sea level rise
Source: IPCC Special Report on Oceans
💰

Economic Cost

India loses an estimated 5.4% of GDP to extreme weather and heat stress — among the highest in the world. Outdoor labour productivity has already dropped 15% since 2000 due to heat. By 2070, unmitigated warming could reduce India's GDP by 24–35% relative to a no-warming scenario.

5.4% GDP lost to climate impacts
Source: RBI Bulletin 2023, Cambridge study
🧲

Health Impacts

Rising heat increases vector-borne diseases: dengue transmission potential has risen 15% since the 1950s. Air pollution — worsened by heat-driven ozone formation — already causes 1.6 million deaths annually. Heat stroke, kidney disease in outdoor workers, and malnutrition from crop failures compound the burden on an overstretched healthcare system.

1.6M air pollution deaths/year
Source: Lancet Countdown, GBD 2021
🏙️

Urban Heat Islands

Indian cities are 3–5°C hotter than surrounding rural areas due to concrete, lost green cover, and waste heat. Delhi, Ahmedabad, and Nagpur regularly cross 47°C. The urban poor — living in tin-roofed homes without cooling — bear the worst of it. India will need 1.1 billion cooling devices by 2037, creating a massive energy demand spiral.

3–5°C urban heat island effect
Source: TERI, India Cooling Action Plan

The Window Is Narrowing

India's National Action Plan on Climate Change targets 50% non-fossil electricity by 2030 and net-zero by 2070. But current trajectories show emissions still rising. The data on this page isn't abstract — it's the lived reality of heat that kills, water that's disappearing, and food that's harder to grow. Every fraction of a degree matters.
